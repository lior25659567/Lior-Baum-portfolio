# Director Verdict — WizeCare

*Reducing Clinician Friction in Home Physical Therapy*

---

### Career Narrative

There IS a thread here, and it's a good one — when it's not buried under `[FILL IN]` brackets. The closing line ("Designing systems that respect the people who use them") and the recurring move of *reusing one card system across three flows so nothing needs retraining* point at a real superpower: **this designer reduces cognitive load in high-stakes clinical workflows by making new tools feel like tools the user already knows.** That's a specific, sellable point of view — friction-elimination over feature-richness, stated explicitly in Goal 1 and Slide 4.

But the narrative undercuts itself in two ways. First, the most senior thinking — the card-system reuse decision, the inline-editing-over-modals call, the "couldn't hide info, only reorganize it" constraint — is real and present, but it's scattered into `highlight` footnotes and `afterDescription` paragraphs rather than positioned as the spine. Second, the case study can't yet answer "what kind of designer is this?" because the single most important seniority field — **the role in Slide 0 — is literally still a placeholder** (`[FILL IN: Lead, Solo, Product?]`). A reviewer cannot tell if they made these decisions or executed someone else's. That one blank is doing more damage than any prose problem in the deck.

It also reads as a one-off, not a body of work. Healthcare/clinical SaaS is clearly the lane — every signal points there — but nothing tells me whether this is *the* clinical case or one of several. As a standalone artifact it's coherent; as a career statement it needs the portfolio around it to confirm the specialization.

### Case Study Architecture

**Hook: 8/10.** "Physiotherapists lose 30% of their day to administrative friction" is a genuinely strong opening — quantified, human, stakes-forward. Sarah's quote ("I waste 15 minutes per patient hunting through screens. By lunch I'm already behind schedule") earns the slot. This is how you start a case study. It does NOT start with "I was tasked with." The only drag: the intro slide itself (Slide 0) opens with bracketed placeholders, so the *actual* first thing a reviewer reads is a broken sentence. Slide 1 should be doing the hooking, and it is — fix Slide 0 so it doesn't sabotage the runway.

**Stakes: 7/10.** Clear *why it's painful* (30% of the day, 12+ patients, three systems, abandon-anything-that-adds-steps). Weaker on *why now* — there's no triggering event, no "adoption was cratering / a competitor shipped / the contract was at risk." The stakes are user-pain stakes, not business stakes. For a B2B SaaS redesign, the missing sentence is "and here's what it was costing WizeCare." The 80%-of-previous-non-users-now-active outcome implies a real adoption crisis was the trigger — say that up front.

**Conflict: 6/10.** This is the deck's structural weakness, and it's hidden in plain sight. The single best conflict beat in the whole study is on Slide 3/4: *clinicians had normalized the friction so completely they didn't report it as a problem — so self-reported research would have lied to you, and you had to switch to direct observation.* That is a real, senior, methodology-altering pivot. It is currently stated calmly as a finding rather than dramatized as the turning point it is. Beyond that one insight, the conflict is thin: there are constraint mentions (12-week timeline, legacy backend, the animation trade-off) and one named trade-off (cut custom exercise animation to ship), but no *failure*, no *dead-end direction*, no *thing that tested badly and got rethought*. A redesign where everything went right is not believable at senior level. The two `process` slides claim validation but every number in them is `[FILL IN]` — so the reader never sees the iteration loop that would supply the missing tension.

**Resolution: 7/10.** The outcomes slide circles back cleanly to the three breakdowns and the original 30%-of-the-day promise (10–15 min saved, 75% fewer misclicks, 80% adoption among former non-users, 55% drop in tickets). Those are strong, specific, and on-theme. Two credibility risks: (1) sourcing is soft — "per stakeholder report," "observed in usability tests" with no n — and four clean round-ish numbers with no methodology invites the "are these real?" reflex; (2) the deck ends on `outcomes → end` with no reflection, so the resolution lands as "it worked" rather than "here's what I now know." That's a junior-shaped ending on senior-shaped work.

**Voice: 6/10.** Inconsistent, and revealingly so. Slides 10 and 13 switch to first person and the writing comes alive — *"Editing a plan shouldn't require a map — so I flattened it into one screen,"* *"Inline editing replaced modal overlays because therapists said 'I lose context when things pop up.'"* That's a designer thinking out loud, making calls, owning them. But most of the deck hides behind "we," and several slides are still skeletal placeholder prose. The person is most present exactly where the work is most senior — which tells you the fix is to extend that first-person, decision-owning voice across the whole deck, not invent it.

**Weakest element: Conflict.** The story has tension available (the normalized-friction pivot, the ship-vs-flexibility trade-offs, the legacy-backend constraint) but never stages it as drama or shows a single thing going *wrong*. Fix this and the case jumps a full seniority tier.

### Seniority Positioning Gap

**Current signal: Mid (reading toward Senior in flashes).** The default voice, the "here's my process" framing of Slides 4–6, and the placeholder role all read mid-level. But Slides 10 and 13 — owned decisions, explicit trade-offs, rationale tied to user quotes, the card-reuse systems-thinking — are unmistakably Senior.

**Target should be: Senior.** Everything about the subject matter (B2B clinical SaaS, multi-flow redesign, adoption outcomes) and the quality of the best slides says this person is operating at senior level and should be claiming it.

**Gap — three concrete closes:**
1. **Fill the role field with a senior title and own it in first person across the deck.** "Solo Designer" or "Lead Product Designer" on Slide 0 changes the read of every decision that follows. Right now the strongest decisions are unattributed.
2. **Add a `reflection` slide.** Senior is "here's the problem and the trade-offs I navigated" *plus* the ability to critique your own work. The animation cut, the things you couldn't measure (long-term retention, true error-rate baseline), and what you'd test next are all already implied — surface them.
3. **Add a `directions` (Ideation) slide for the Protocol Builder.** The Kanban builder currently appears as a finished answer. Show the options you rejected and *why* — that's the difference between "I made a builder" and "I navigated a design decision."

### Market Positioning

**Optimized for: healthcare / clinical-SaaS product teams and B2B workflow-tool companies** — Align/iTero-adjacent medtech, EHR and care-platform companies, digital-health startups. The vocabulary (clinicians, contextual inquiry, adherence data, care plans, protocol builders, adoption among non-users) is exactly the dialect those hiring managers screen for. A clinical design lead would recognize this as "our kind of designer" instantly. That's a valuable, defensible niche — lean into it.

**Who would pass, and why:** Consumer / brand / 0-to-1 product shops looking for visual range and craft storytelling — this deck is all workflow logic and almost no shown design craft (every image is a caption like "New — dedicated patient page," never a hero visual that makes you *feel* the improvement). A design-systems-heavy enterprise team might also want more rigor evidence than "per stakeholder report." And any reviewer at any company will pass on the `[FILL IN]` brackets alone — in current state this is an unfinished draft, and unfinished reads as "doesn't ship polished work," which is fatal for a craft discipline.

---

### The Signature Move

**It's there, and it's strong: "one mental model, reused across every flow, so the new tool needs zero retraining."** The card system spanning the patient view, the care-plan editor, AND the protocol builder — explicitly justified as "validated by reusing existing mental models" and "felt familiar from day one" — is a genuine systems-design signature. Almost nobody articulates adoption-through-familiarity this cleanly. It's currently buried in two `afterDescription` paragraphs. Pull it out, name it as the thesis of the whole redesign, and it becomes the thing a reviewer remembers in a stack of 40. Without that framing, this is a competent-but-forgettable "I cleaned up a clinical dashboard" case. With it, it's "the designer who makes new tools feel like old ones."

### 3 Rewrites That Would Change Everything

1. **Fill Slide 0's role field with a senior title and rewrite the description as one owned, quantified sentence — no brackets.** e.g. *"As [Lead/Solo] Designer over a 12-week sprint, I rebuilt WizeCare's clinician workflows around a single reusable card system — lifting daily adoption among former non-users to 80%."* This single fix moves the seniority read more than anything else in the deck.

2. **Promote the "normalized friction" insight (Slide 3/4) from a calm finding to the staged turning point of the case.** Make it a `quotes` slide carrying the clinician verbatims, then a one-line pivot: *"They'd stopped seeing the problem — so I stopped asking and started watching."* That converts your strongest piece of thinking from a bullet into the conflict the whole study is missing.

3. **Add `directions` (Ideation) before the Protocol Builder comparison, and `reflection` before the `end` slide.** The first shows judgment (what you rejected for the builder and why); the second shows maturity (the animation cut, what you couldn't measure, what you'd test next). Together they close the entire mid→senior gap — these are the two beats whose absence is currently capping the read.

---

### Director Verdict: **Strong Draft**

The single biggest opportunity: the senior thinking is all already here — owned decisions, a real research pivot, a genuine systems-design signature — but it's hidden in placeholder brackets and footnote `highlight` fields; fill the role, surface the card-system thesis, and add the two missing beats (`directions` + `reflection`), and this jumps from competent mid-level to a memorable senior clinical-SaaS case.

> **Flag for Recruiter:** The intro slide's role field is an unfilled placeholder — a screener cannot determine seniority or ownership in the 6-second scan, which is disqualifying regardless of how good the work is. Multiple `[FILL IN]` brackets throughout make the deck read as unfinished, which signals "doesn't ship polished work." Outcome metrics are sourced as "per stakeholder report" with no sample sizes — strong numbers, soft credibility. Positioning is sharp (clinical/B2B SaaS) once these are fixed.

> **Flag for UX Reviewer:** Two `process` slides (6 and 14) are identical and both entirely placeholder — the iteration/validation evidence that proves the redesign worked is missing, so the outcomes float unsupported. No ideation/divergence shown, so the Kanban builder and consolidated patient view read as arbitrary rather than reasoned. Voice flips between "we" (flat) and "I" (alive) — standardize on first-person decision ownership, which is where the craft already shows. Comparison slides describe the before/after in words but never let the visual carry the transformation.
