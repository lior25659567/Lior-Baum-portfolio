# Recruiter Verdict — WizeCare

### 4-Minute First Impression

The first thing that stands out — positively — is the framing. "Physiotherapists lose 30% of their day to administrative friction" is a sharp, business-legible opening, and the structure is clean: context → problem → research → goals → three chaptered deep-dives (Patient Management, Care Plan Editing, Protocol Builder) → outcomes. As a recruiter skimming, I can follow exactly what happened without working for it. That alone puts it ahead of most.

The narrative is immediately clear. The three-breakdown spine (patient screen, care plan editing, protocol creation) is set up early and paid off consistently — each chapter resolves one. That kind of structural discipline reads as someone who thinks in systems, not screens.

The most memorable thing an hour later: the Protocol Builder slide, where they replaced rigid templates with a drag-and-drop Kanban and explicitly reused the card system from the care-plan editor for "zero learning curve." That's a real design decision with a rationale, and it's the moment the designer becomes visible.

What I'd forget — and what nags immediately — is the staggering number of `[FILL IN: ...]` placeholders. The very first slide doesn't even state the person's role, timeline, or a single outcome. The most important sentence in any case study — "here's what I did and what it changed" — is literally a blank. For a finished portfolio this would be an instant pass; I'm reading this as a strong draft, and I'll judge the thinking on display while flagging the gaps honestly.

### Role & Level Signal

This signals a **mid-to-senior product designer (IC)** optimized for **B2B SaaS, healthcare/clinical, or workflow-heavy enterprise tools**. The tells: comfort with dense information design (6+ data points visible at a glance), respect for legacy backend constraints, contextual-inquiry research, and an instinct for adoption over feature richness. This is someone who has clearly worked on a real product with real engineering limits, not a Dribbble redesign.

They'd thrive on a focused product team with a PM and a couple of engineers where they own a workflow end-to-end. They'd struggle in a pure visual/brand role or a giant org where they're one of fifteen designers shipping fragments — the strength here is owning a whole problem.

The work shows someone who **executes design with strong product judgment**, leaning toward leading the design of a feature area. It does not yet show someone who leads other designers.

### Red Flags

- **Pervasive missing data.** Roughly a third of the impactful fields are `[FILL IN]` — role, timeline, sample sizes, validation methods, task-time deltas. The case is currently un-finishable as presented.
- **Role ambiguity is the worst offender.** Slide 0 never says whether they were solo, lead, or one of several. The prose flips between "we" (research, goals) and "I" (Slide 10: "so I flattened it"; Slide 13 "I"). A recruiter can't tell what this person personally did versus what the team did. This is the single most damaging gap.
- **Outcomes look quantified but are unsupported.** Slide 15 lists 75% misclick reduction, 80% daily-active among former non-users, 55% support-ticket drop — yet the validation slides (6 and 14) are entirely `[FILL IN]` for method and sample size, and those two process slides are duplicates of each other. Hard numbers with no described measurement reads as fabricated or aspirational until proven. That's a credibility risk, not a strength.
- **Key research quotes are placeholders.** Slide 3's "key insight" is a `[FILL IN]` for the actual clinician quotes — the emotional core is missing.
- **Testimonial attribution is thin.** "Clinic interviews / WizeCare user research, 2023" is not a named person — fine as a research note, weak as social proof.

### Green Flags

- **Genuine research-to-design traceability.** 6 clinic observations, 12 interviews, 89 support tickets → three breakdown points → four goals → three chaptered solutions. Each design decision ties back to an observed behavior. This is the strongest signal in the case.
- **Constraint navigation is explicit and mature.** Every design goal names its constraint: 12-week timeline, legacy backend, no net-new data models, "couldn't hide info, only reorganize." This is the rarest thing in a junior portfolio and the clearest seniority marker here.
- **Honest trade-offs.** Slide 10: "I sacrificed custom animation options to ship within [timeline] — planned to revisit." Naming what was cut and deferred is exactly what separates a real practitioner from a portfolio polisher.
- **A clear point of view.** After reading, I know what this designer believes: tools must remove steps, not add them; familiarity drives adoption; respect the user's existing mental models. The closing line — "Designing systems that respect the people who use them" — is earned by the body of work, not bolted on.
- **Business-and-user fluency.** They care about adoption, support-ticket cost, and clinician time, not just aesthetics.

### Level Calibration

Realistically supports a **strong L4 (mid) / borderline L5 (senior)** offer — *if* the placeholders are filled with real data. The thinking on display (constraint articulation, research synthesis, trade-off honesty) is L5-caliber. What holds it back from a clean senior call is twofold: (1) the unresolved I-vs-we ownership question, and (2) unsubstantiated impact metrics. A senior candidate must be able to say "I owned X, measured it Y way, and it moved Z." Right now the case asserts Z without Y and is fuzzy on who owns X.

The single thing missing that would push them to the next level: **one credible, defended outcome** — a real before/after task-time number with the test that produced it, clearly attributed to their decision.

### Culture Fit Profile

Best environment: a **mid-stage B2B/healthtech startup or a focused product pod** in a larger company — somewhere with enough ambiguity to use their research instincts but a real product and real constraints to push against. They show ownership instincts and comfort with messy, legacy-constrained reality, which suits startup ambiguity over enterprise process. They appear collaborative (the "we" of research) rather than lone-genius, though the writing should commit to that framing instead of drifting between pronouns. Intellectual curiosity is present — they observed workflows rather than trusting self-reported pain, which is a genuinely sophisticated research move.

### Would You Advance Them?

**Maybe** — leaning yes the moment the `[FILL IN]` role line and at least one substantiated metric are filled in; as it literally stands, the missing role and unsupported numbers are screen-out risks.

### Recruiter Verdict: Maybe

The thinking is clearly Advance-grade, but a portfolio that doesn't state the designer's own role and props up impressive metrics on entirely blank validation slides can't be advanced until those specific gaps are closed.

> **Flag for UX Reviewer:** Slides 6 and 14 are identical ("How we validated the redesign") and both fully blank — likely a duplication error; the actual validation methodology is the load-bearing missing piece for the whole case. Also worth a craft eye on whether 17 slides could compress (two chapter+comparison pairs are structurally repetitive).
> **Flag for Director:** The I/we pronoun inconsistency and the absent role statement are positioning problems that materially hurt candidacy — this person likely did senior-level work but is narrating it in a way that obscures their ownership. Coaching them to commit to a clear "I led / I owned" frame and back the four headline metrics would move this from Maybe to Advance.
