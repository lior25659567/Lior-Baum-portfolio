# Director Verdict — The Techno Chronicles (project-1776617213367)

## Career Narrative

This deck presents a personal creative project — a scroll-telling experience built from Wikipedia content on techno music. As a standalone artifact the visual work is clearly confident: strong typographic hierarchy, a dark editorial aesthetic, atmospheric textures, deliberate scroll-based layout. The site looks like the work of someone with real craft.

But as a career narrative aimed at a Senior SaaS Product Designer role, the deck does not land. There is no coherent thread connecting it to Lior's stated superpower — "takes complex, intimidating workflows and makes them feel simple and familiar." This is a solo creative expression project with no user problem, no business constraint, and no measurable outcome. Craft range without a decision-making story does not answer the Senior hiring question: "Can this person own a product workflow end-to-end?"

After seven slides, the superpower that comes through is "aesthetic control under a self-imposed creative brief." Not wrong — but not the positioning Lior is building toward.

## Case Study Architecture

**Hook: 5/10** — The intro description ("breaking the generic layout to capture the raw energy of techno") is the most evocative line in the deck and earns attention, but works for a design blog, not a product portfolio. `metaItems` (Role, Timeline, Team, Tools) is missing entirely, stripping every seniority signal before slide two. `headlineMetric` is empty.

**Stakes: 2/10** — The biggest structural failure. No stated problem with real stakes — no user struggling, no business cost, no constraint creating meaningful design tension. "Capture techno without overdoing it" is a taste judgment, not a conflict with consequences.

**Conflict: 3/10** — Slide 2's highlight ("The vibe had to live in the rhythm — not the decoration") is a genuine, well-stated design constraint, but it lives in one sentence and is never developed. No paths explored and rejected, no `directions` slide, no iteration evidence, no before-and-after. A case study with no documented failure is not believable to a senior reviewer.

**Resolution: 4/10** — The `media` slide has no caption/description/highlight. The `outcomes` slide repurposes its metric fields (Rhythm, Balance, Responsive, Clarity) as design principles, not outcomes. The resolution never circles back to the original premise because the premise was never specific enough.

**Voice: 6/10** — The strongest element. "The vibe had to live in the rhythm — not the decoration" and "Not a redesign of Wikipedia — a reimagining of how its content could feel" are compressed, specific, confident. The personality is present. But strong voice without strong substance reads as style over thinking — the wrong signal for a Senior role.

**Weakest element: Stakes.** No user, no consequence, no cost-of-failure. Without that, every decision reads as personal preference. Fix first: honestly frame what this project is (a self-initiated craft exploration) and explain precisely what it proves that the product work does not.

## Seniority Positioning Gap

**Current signal: Junior/Mid.** The deck shows what was made and gestures at creative intent but never surfaces decisions, tradeoffs, or judgment calls. The `outcomes` slide lists design philosophy dressed as learnings. The missing `reflection` slide is the clearest indicator: a senior designer critiques their own work publicly.

**Target should be: Senior.** Every case study must read as "Here is the problem I solved and the tradeoffs I navigated."

**Gap: two changes close it.** (1) Add a `reflection` slide — a personal project gives latitude for an honest one ("I had no user research; here is what I would do differently as a product problem"). (2) Convert one of the three consecutive `problem` slides into a `directions` (Ideation) slide — show paths considered, name what was rejected and why.

## Market Positioning

This deck is optimized for a creative agency audience or design blog readership. A creative director at an editorial studio or motion design house would be impressed. B2B SaaS / workflow / clinical healthtech product teams would immediately pass — there is no user, no workflow, and the constraints are entirely aesthetic and self-imposed.

Specific risk: placed alongside the iTero or WizeCare work, this deck creates a genre mismatch that disrupts the coherent positioning arc. The intro slide must make explicit what this project proves that the product work does not.

## The Signature Move

There is a buried signature: "I can translate genre — not just visual style, but the felt experience of a cultural artifact — into scroll-based UI rhythm." The line "The vibe had to live in the rhythm — not the decoration" gestures at a real philosophy: restraint as amplification. The differentiated skill is encoding non-visual experiences (rhythm, energy, pace) into UI behavior. It is stated once, never developed, never connected to transferable decisions. The signature needs to become the thesis of the case study, not a highlighted aside.

## 3 Rewrites That Would Change Everything

**1. Rewrite Slide 0 description and populate `metaItems` + `headlineMetric`.** Front-load what the project proves: "No brief, no client, no user research — so I built my own constraints: make the information feel like the music sounds." Add `metaItems` (Role: Solo Designer; Timeline; Context: Self-initiated; Tools: Figma + Webflow).

**2. Convert one of the three `problem` slides into a `directions` (Ideation) slide.** Slide 3 (Responsive) is the weakest as problem-framing. Replace Slides 2-3 with one `directions` slide: Direction A (typographic maximalism — rejected, decoration competes with content), Direction B (ambient minimalism — rejected, disappears on scroll), Direction C (rhythmic reveal through scroll pacing — accepted). Showing rejected paths is the fastest senior-judgment signal in a personal deck.

**3. Add a `reflection` slide before the `end`.** Name one transferable skill ("encoding pace and hierarchy through scroll timing is the same cognitive problem as rationing attention in a multi-step workflow"); one constraint you'd change ("I'd prototype three scroll-speed variations with five users before committing — I shipped on intuition"); one thing you couldn't measure ("whether someone who knows nothing about techno still feels the genre's energy"). That last sentence signals more maturity than the entire current `outcomes` slide.

## Director Verdict: Needs Rework

Visual craft is evident, voice is present, and the underlying idea — encoding rhythm as UI behavior — is memorable. But the deck reads as a creative journal entry rather than a Senior design argument, with no user, no stakes, no documented iteration, and no `reflection` slide.

> **Flag for Recruiter:** Invisible in a SaaS product screen — no user problem, no measurable outcome, no context connecting it to product design thinking. Rewrite the intro to make the transfer case explicit.

> **Flag for UX Reviewer:** Three consecutive `textAndImage` problem slides over-allocate to premise-setting while the process is invisible — no iteration evidence, no `directions`/Ideation slide, no before-and-after, and a `media` slide with zero editorial framing. The `outcomes` slide misuses its metric fields as brand-voice principles. The `reflection` template is absent and required above mid-level.
