# Recruiter Verdict — The Techno Chronicles (project-1776617213367)

## 4-Minute First Impression

The visual work is immediately arresting. The hero image — stark black, massive white type, a halftone sphere — telegraphs creative confidence in about two seconds. By the time you reach the scroll-telling screenshots (broken-grid text layouts, ASCII-style portraits of techno pioneers, the Berghain tunnel), you understand this person can translate an aesthetic brief into something genuinely felt, not just decorated.

But the narrative collapses the moment you look for substance. What is this case study actually about? A personal project. A Webflow scroll site. That's all we learn. The "problem" is three back-to-back textAndImage slides (Slides 1, 2, 3) that describe the same creative challenge from different angles — energy, vibe, responsiveness — with no meaningful differentiation between them. There is no research, no defined audience, no constraints named, no iteration shown, no outcomes measured, and no reflection. The "Key Learnings" slide delivers four generic design principles that could have been written by anyone after any visual project.

An hour later, you would remember the visuals. You would not remember a single decision Lior made.

## Role & Level Signal

This reads as a portfolio piece for a visual/interaction designer at an agency or a motion-forward consumer studio — not a Senior Product Designer at a B2B SaaS company. Every sentence is about aesthetic intent. No sentence is about a user, a workflow, a stakeholder, a constraint, or a business outcome.

For the target — Senior Product Designer at a mid-size SaaS company with workflow-heavy products — this case study is actively misaligned. SaaS hiring managers look for evidence that a designer understands complexity, tradeoffs, and systems. This study shows visual taste on a personal project.

Company fit signal: agency, consumer brand, editorial/publishing design, motion studio. Not B2B SaaS.

## Red Flags

- No process shown — only final polished screens. Zero wireframes, zero iterations, no before/after.
- All work appears solo — no collaboration, no engineering constraints, no PM, no user, no stakeholder.
- Vague impact: "Key Learnings" are four generic design principles with no measurement or validation.
- All concept/personal work — a Webflow personal project; no shipped commercial product.
- Reads like a creative brief, not a case study: no "I chose X over Y because..." anywhere.
- Overuse of vague aesthetic framing: "raw energy," "the vibe had to live in the rhythm," "bold accents."
- No metaItems on the intro slide — no role, timeline, team, or tools declared.
- No reflection slide — required above mid-level and by the profile's seniority target.
- The `outcomes` template is misused: metric fields (Rhythm, Balance, Responsive, Clarity) are category labels, not values.
- Missing canonical beats: Research Overview (3), Key Insights (4), Iteration Evidence (6), Outcome/Impact with real data (8).

## Green Flags

- The visual execution is genuinely distinctive — atmospheric, cohesive, not generic dribbble work.
- The scroll-telling concept is well-chosen and confidently executed (broken grid, ASCII art, perspective tunnels, timeline infographics).
- The intro description is clean and specific: "breaking the generic layout to capture the raw energy of techno."
- Slide word counts are all within budget — economical writing.
- Visual range across the five intro images demonstrates real range and scroll-pacing understanding.

## Level Calibration

Realistic level supported by this case study: L3 Junior / early L4 Mid — on the visual execution dimension only. No evidence of senior-level product thinking: no tradeoffs, no constraints, no user context, no measurement, no reflection.

What would push this to Senior signal:
1. Add metaItems — role, timeline, tools.
2. Add a reflection slide — 2-3 honest sentences on what the project taught and what you'd do differently.
3. Fix the "Key Learnings" slide — real outcomes or retype to reflection.
4. Attach a "because" to at least one decision.

## Culture Fit Profile

Strong fit for: consumer-facing products, editorial platforms, creative agencies, design-forward startups in entertainment/culture.

For the stated target (B2B SaaS, workflow-heavy, product-led): this case study does not help. If Techno Chronicles stays, it needs explicit framing as a side project demonstrating a specific named skill (scroll-telling interaction design, expressive editorial typography, Webflow motion design) — not positioned as a peer to the clinical workflow case studies.

## Would You Advance Them?

Maybe — this study alone is a pass for the Senior SaaS target, but alongside two strong clinical workflow case studies (iTero, WizeCare), it becomes an acceptable range/personality piece. Adding metaItems, a genuine reflection slide, and fixing the "Key Learnings" slide (~60 words) moves it from "personal project showcase" to "deliberate portfolio piece with a point of view."

## Recruiter Verdict: Maybe

As a visual range piece nested inside a portfolio with two stronger product case studies, this is acceptable — as a standalone or primary signal for a Senior SaaS target, it is a pass.

> **Flag for UX Reviewer:** The `outcomes` template is being misused — the metric fields (Rhythm, Balance, Responsive, Clarity) are category labels, not values. Consider retyping to `reflection`. Three consecutive `problem`-type slides with near-identical cadence could collapse into one `issuesBreakdown` slide.

> **Flag for Director:** Right now this study lives in the portfolio as if equivalent to the clinical workflow studies. Decide: credibility/range piece (needs explicit framing as a personal side project demonstrating a named skill), or move it to a secondary "explorations" section / cut it from the Senior SaaS-targeting portfolio. Current positioning is neutral at best, dilutive at worst.
