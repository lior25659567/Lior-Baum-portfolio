# Director Verdict — Patient Report Redesign (project-1776014998709)

### Career narrative
The stated superpower ("I make complex workflows feel obvious") is actually demonstrated by this project — a tool doctors skipped because it started blank. The work shows a designer who thinks about defaults and the moment before the user starts. What's missing is the growth question: read alone, it's competent execution of a well-scoped problem, not yet a designer who escalated scope, pushed back on direction, or changed how the team worked. The superpower lands at the feature level, not the process/system level.

### Case study architecture
- **Hook 8/10** — specific, human, real insight ("default state as the problem"); transfers beyond clinical. Missing: business stakes in the hook.
- **Stakes 4/10 (weakest)** — never says why this mattered at scale or what it cost (retention? clinical quality? adoption?). Research is underweight (no count/method). Outcomes are unanchored adjectives.
- **Conflict 6/10** — `directions` slide is the strongest senior signal (rejected paths solved symptoms; accepted solved root cause). Reflection adds honest process conflict (annotation scoped late → rework; sharing perms defined late). But the research→goals arc is frictionless (4 quotes → 4 goals, nothing cut) — reads slightly sanitized.
- **Resolution 7/10** — structurally closes the loops opened early; before/after comparisons are visual and specific. Weak link: outcomes ask the reader to take impact on faith.
- **Voice 7/10** — first-person, decision-owning ("I moved everything into one header line"). Goes quiet exactly on the outcomes slide where confidence is most needed.
- **Weakest: Stakes** — no business framing; reads as good execution, not business-aware design.

### Seniority positioning gap
Current: **Mid trending Senior.** Directions + reflection read Senior; the rest reads high-Mid. Two changes close most of it: (1) a **stakes sentence** (what incomplete reports cost — even non-numeric: "flagged by product leadership as a retention risk"); (2) a **"what this enabled next" sentence** (did the modular block system become the baseline / get reused?) — the difference between "I shipped a redesign" and "I changed how this product area works."

### Market positioning
Optimized for B2B clinical SaaS — compelling there. A non-clinical SaaS manager may screen it as "dental software designer" because the transfer argument is only one sentence on the end card. Add an explicit frame in the intro/problem: clinical reporting = an extreme version of any high-stakes professional workflow. (Profile target SaaS framing is `confirmed: false` — making the transfer explicit matters more if the target shifts general.)

### The signature move
Almost-articulated: **fix the default state** — "make the right thing the easy thing." Present in the hook, hinted in goals, named only in the reflection's closing line. Introduce it in the problem slide and reference it in goals + reflection → a memorable through-line instead of a feature-by-feature walkthrough.

### 3 rewrites that would change everything
1. **Add business stakes to the problem/intro** — one sentence on what the blank-page problem cost.
2. **Reframe the goals slide around the philosophy** — "what should already be done for the doctor?" so the 4 goals read as one principle.
3. **Ground the outcomes** — at least one real number or a more explicit early-access context (N doctors, period); amplify the testimonial.

### Slide ORDER — structural problem
Current ends `…10 Outcomes · 11 end · 12 Reflection`. **Reflection (12) is after the end card (11)** — unreachable in normal navigation, invisible as a seniority signal. **`move slide 12 to after slide 10`** → Outcomes → Reflection → end. (Secondary, optional: testimonial (9) could move after outcomes — current position is fine; the reflection error is the priority.)

### Director Verdict: Strong Draft
Great hook, real senior signal in the directions slide, signature move almost there — but it asks managers to evaluate a clinical redesign without saying what was at risk, and buries the seniority-defining reflection after the end card.

> **Flag for Recruiter:** Reads as a clinical specialist piece without a transfer argument — add one explicit clinical→general-SaaS frame.
> **Flag for UX Reviewer:** Reflection (12) sequenced after end (11) — move before end. Outcomes have no numeric anchors / no early-access N; research gives no interview count or method.
